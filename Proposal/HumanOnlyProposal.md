## Project 1: Alpha-M (The MLFF Benchmark)

**The question:** Do machine-learning force fields produce realistic protein dynamics?

**Why it matters:** MLFFs (MACE-OFF24, SO3LR, etc.) are validated only against quantum
chemistry calculations. Nobody has tested whether their MD trajectories match
*experimental* NMR measurements like S2 order parameters. Classical force fields have
been refined against NMR for 30 years. MLFFs? Never tested. This is the "reality gap"
exposed for materials MLFFs by UniFFBench -- we do it for biology.

**What you do:**
- Run 10 different ensemble generators (2 MLFFs, 1 ML-classical, 3 generative models,
  4 classical force fields) on 14 proteins
- Compare their dynamics against experimental NMR S2 order parameters
- Rank them: which produces the most physically realistic protein dynamics?

**The key proteins:** 14 proteins organized by size. Small ones (<50 residues: WW domain,
crambin, GB3) can be simulated with MLFFs. Medium ones (50-80 res: ubiquitin, BPTI, CI2,
etc.) are the interesting frontier. Large ones (>80 res: barnase, HEWL, T4 lysozyme)
get classical MD and generative models only -- MLFFs can't handle them yet.

**The 10 generators:**
1. MACE-OFF24 (MLFF) and SO3LR (MLFF) -- the exciting unknowns
2. Garnet (NMR-trained ML force field) -- treated as a contamination case study since
   5/7 of its benchmark proteins were in its training set
3. BioEmu, Boltz-2, AlphaFlow (generative models) -- fast, no dynamics simulation
4. ff14SB, ff19SB, a99SB-disp, CHARMM36m (classical) -- the established baselines

**What was dropped:** AI2BMD was removed. All 5 reviewers agreed: 22 open GitHub issues,
no H200 GPU support, Docker-only (no Singularity for HPC), and its hybrid water model
(classical water + ML protein) makes it a poor "pure MLFF" representative. Zero
scientific loss from dropping it.

**Risk you should know about:** There's a 25% chance neither MLFF produces stable
trajectories >5 ns on any protein. If that happens, you still have a publishable
benchmark of classical + generative methods at NatMeth, but with reduced scope.

**GO/NO-GO: June 30.** After 8 weeks of pilot simulations. At least 1 MLFF must
produce a stable >10 ns trajectory on at least 3 proteins.

**Compute:** ~167K GPU-hours total. The big number. Most of this is Phase 3 replicas.
Phase 2 production is ~24-34K GPU-hours.

## Project 2: Gamma (Dynamics Predicts Fitness)

**The question:** Do conformational dynamics features improve mutation fitness prediction
beyond what you get from sequence conservation alone?

**Why it matters:** ProteinGym has 97+ methods for predicting whether a mutation is
harmful. Zero of them use explicit dynamics. RSALOR (residue conservation + solvent
accessibility) sits at rank #15 with Spearman 0.465 using essentially zero compute.
Can dynamics -- generated by BioEmu in minutes -- add anything?

**What you do:**
- Generate BioEmu conformational ensembles for 150+ ProteinGym proteins
- Extract dynamics features (RMSF, flexibility, contacts, etc.)
- Train ML models (MLP, XGBoost, GATv2) with ESM2 + RSA + dynamics features
- Test whether adding dynamics beats RSALOR

**The central experiment:**
```
RSALOR                   (Spearman 0.465, the baseline)
RSALOR + RMSF            (does dynamics add anything?)
```
If RSALOR+RMSF > RSALOR: dynamics provides genuinely new information. Publishable.
If RSALOR+RMSF = RSALOR: dynamics is just re-encoding conservation. Still publishable
as a well-powered negative result.

**Where dynamics should help most:**
- Binding assays (RSALOR only 0.416) -- conformational selection matters
- Activity/catalysis assays (RSALOR 0.479) -- active site flexibility matters
- NOT stability assays (RSALOR 0.575) -- dynamics doesn't help here

**The ceiling problem:** BioEmu is trained on AMBER ff14SB MD. ff14SB itself only
achieves R^2 = 0.62 vs experimental NMR. So BioEmu dynamics preserve only ~49% of
the true dynamics signal. The realistic improvement over RSALOR is +0.04-0.06
Spearman in the best case, +0.02-0.03 in the moderate case. It's detectable but
not huge.

**Compute:** ~2,000 GPU-hours. Trivial.

**Scoop risk:** 35-45%. The Microsoft BioEmu team could connect BioEmu to ProteinGym
at any time. Monthly competition scans are planned.

## Project 3: Delta (PerturbMark)

**The question:** When does deep learning actually outperform simple linear baselines
for predicting cellular perturbation responses?

**Why it matters:** The field is in crisis. Some papers say DL fails (Ahlmann-Eltze,
NatMeth 2025). Others say DL wins (Miller, bioRxiv 2025). The difference is mostly
which metrics you use -- uncalibrated metrics make DL look bad, calibrated metrics
make it look good. Nobody has resolved this on the world's largest dataset (Tahoe-100M:
100M cells, 50 cell lines, 379 compounds). And nobody has done it neutrally -- existing
benchmarks are either from dataset creators (SCALE, Tahoe-x1) or use small datasets
(scPerturBench).

**What you do:**
- Run 5 DL methods + 5 baselines on Tahoe-100M
- Evaluate with 7 calibrated metrics (not the broken ones the field has been using)
- Stratify by difficulty: Tier 0 (easy, in-distribution) through Tier 3 (hard, unseen
  perturbation in new cell type)
- Answer: under what conditions does DL help, and by how much?

**The methods:** GEARS, scGPT, CPA, scFoundation, and Tahoe-x1 (the dataset creators'
own model -- omitting it would be a glaring gap). Plus 5 simple baselines including
linear regression and a random control.

**Why this is time-sensitive:** NVIDIA's SCALE paper (March 2026) already establishes
some Tahoe-100M baselines, but it's a self-evaluation, not neutral. If someone publishes
an independent Tahoe-100M benchmark before you, Delta's value drops. Preprint by
August 15 is non-negotiable.

**Either outcome is publishable:**
- DL wins under calibrated metrics: resolves the controversy in DL's favor
- DL still loses at 100M scale: extends Ahlmann-Eltze ("even scale doesn't help") --
  equally significant

**Compute:** ~15-20K GPU-hours. Most methods run on RTX 5000 Ada (32 GB, don't need
H200). Only scFoundation and Tahoe-x1 need H200 for fine-tuning.

## The Combined Paper Option (Alpha-M + Gamma -> NCS)

**The claim:** Generators that produce more physically accurate dynamics (better NMR
agreement) also produce more functionally useful predictions (better fitness Spearman).
Better physics = better biology.

**Why it's exciting:** This has literally never been tested. The claim is completely
unoccupied in the literature. If true, it connects two fields (force field development
and protein engineering) that have never been formally linked.

**Why it's risky (30% viability):**
- Statistical power is marginal. The Bayesian test gives BF~3.9 at the expanded
  14x10 design -- barely "moderate evidence"
- BioEmu's ff14SB ceiling attenuates the signal by ~50%
- NCS has published zero force field papers in 2024-2026
- Requires BOTH Alpha-M and Gamma to succeed simultaneously

**The deal:** Run Alpha-M and Gamma separately through August. At August 31, check
whether the integration data supports the combined claim. If yes (rho>0.4, BF>3),
write the combined NCS paper (submit November). If no, split into Alpha-M at NatMeth
and Gamma at Genome Research. You lose nothing by trying.

## What Could Go Wrong (and What Happens)

| Scenario | Probability | What You Get |
|----------|------------|-------------|
| Everything works | 15% | Combined NCS + Delta NatMeth (jackpot) |
| MLFFs partially work, Gamma positive | 50% | Alpha-M NatMeth + Gamma GR + Delta NatMeth |
| MLFF pilot fails, Gamma null | 25% | Alpha-M classical-only (JCTC) + Delta NatMeth |
| Major software failures | 10% | Alpha-M delayed + Delta delayed |
| Complete failure | <2% | Nothing published |

**The portfolio is designed so that even the pessimistic case produces publications.**
Delta is essentially independent and has the highest viability. Alpha-M can always
fall back to a classical-only benchmark. Only a simultaneous failure of all software
stacks produces nothing, and that probability is <2%.
